Traps, Interceptors, Indirect Waste, Vents & DWV
Key Definitions
Critical Terms — Know These Cold
TRAPFitting that provides a water seal (51–102mm deep) to prevent backflow of sewer gas without impeding waste flow. Every fixture needs one.
CROWN WEIRHighest point of bottom of internal trap surface. Trap seal measured from crown weir to top of dip.
DIPLowest portion of inside top surface of the channel through the trap.
TRAP SEALVertical depth of liquid retained — minimum 51 mm, max 102 mm.
INTERCEPTORDevice to separate and retain deleterious matter (grease, sand, oil) from normal wastes.
DWV SYSTEMDrainage + Waste + Vent — the complete building drainage network.
INDIRECT WASTEFixture/appliance drains that do NOT connect directly to soil/waste/vent pipes — separated by air gap or airbreak.
AIRGAPUnobstructed vertical distance between pipe outlet and flood rim of receptor (min. 2× pipe diameter or 25mm, whichever greater).
AIRBREAKPhysical separation with at least 25mm between pipe inlet and receptor.
FOG DISPOSALFats, Oils & Grease disposal — requires grease interceptor in commercial kitchens.
Types of Traps (Residential)
P
P-TrapMost common. Used at lavatories, kitchen sinks, floor drains, scuppers.
D
Drum TrapCylindrical. Used at bathtubs and bidets. Installed under bathroom floor.
H
House/Running TrapPrevents air circulation between building drain and sewer. Installed at base of building drain.
G
Grease Trap/InterceptorRemoves fats/oils from wastewater. NOT required for individual dwellings.
5 Causes of Trap Seal Loss — SEBCW
S
SiphonageLiquid withdrawn by suction from flowing waste. Two types: (a) Direct Self-siphonage, (b) Indirect/Momentum siphonage.
E
EvaporationFixture unused for long time. Deep seal traps help but may clog.
B
Back PressureExcessive pressure at lowest branch blows out trap seal.
C
Capillary AttractionString/rag in trap wicks water out by capillary action.
W
Wind EffectsStrong wind through vent system forces water out of trap.
DWV System Components
Soil Drainage
  • Carries discharge from water closets & urinals (fecal matter)
  • Also receives other fixtures connected to it
  • Soil stack: vertical soil pipe
  • Min. pipe: 100mm Ø (4")
Waste/Sanitary Drainage
  • Liquid waste free of fecal matter
  • From sinks, lavatories, bathtubs, showers
  • Waste stack: vertical waste pipe
  • Waste pipe: carries only wastewater
Storm Drainage
  • Clear water only — rain, surface runoff, ground water
  • NO sanitary waste allowed
  • Leaders/downspouts → catch basin → storm drain
  • Roof gutter → downspout (conductor)

Vent Types (Part 1)

Individual / Back VentServes a single fixture. Most common individual protection.
Unit VentVentilates two similar fixtures on opposite sides of a partition.
Circuit / Loop VentVentilates two or more fixture traps discharging into one soil/waste branch.

Looped VentLoop arrangement of back vent; used where fixture is remote from stack.
Utility VentUsed for less critical fixtures like floor drains.

Grease Interceptor Sizing (Sec. 1011–1013 NPC 1999)
Formula 1 — Scheduled Meals (Cafeteria, Hospital, School)
Grease trap capacity (gallons)
C = M × W × R × F
MNumber of meals at peak hour
WWaste flow rate: with dishwasher=6gal, without=5gal, single service=2gal, food waste disposer=1gal, hospital=25gal/bed/day
RHours of operation (peak)
FStorage factor: 8hr=1, 18hr=2, 24hr=3; single service=1.5
Formula 2 — Non-Scheduled (Restaurants, Fast Food)
C = 0.09 (K × N × H × G × S)
K (Facility)Freeway=1.25, Recreation=1.00, Main highway=0.80, Lesser road=0.5
NNumber of seats
HHours per day operation
GWaste flow rate (general: 4.5 gal)
S (Sewage)To public sewer=1.7, other disposal=2.5
⚡ Sample Problem (Exam Ready)

Canteen: 50 meals/day, open 11am–7pm (8hrs), no dishwasher.

C = 50 × 5 × 2.5 × 1 = 625 gallons
V = 625 ÷ 264 = 2.37 m³
  • M=50 meals, W=5 (no dishwasher), R=2.5hrs peak, F=1 (8hr operation)
  • 1 cubic meter = 264 gallons
  • NOT required for individual dwellings (Sec. 1011.1)
  • NO food waste disposal unit into grease trap (Sec. 1013)
General Requirements — Properly Designed Drainage System
7 Key Requirements
a
Piping must be airtight, gas-tight and watertight.
b
Each fixture (except integral traps) must have a separate water-seal trap.
c
Each trap must have vent pipes to protect against siphonage & back pressure.
d
Cleanouts required: upper end of every horizontal run; every change of direction >22.5°; within 1.5m inside property line; every 15m of horizontal run.
e
Horizontal piping: minimum grade of 2% (2 cm/meter) toward disposal point.
f
Horizontal piping supported and anchored at max 3-meter intervals.
g
Vertical piping secured at close intervals; stacks supported at bases.

Sanitary Drainage, Storm Drainage & Gray Water
Drainage System Types
ONE-PIPE System
  • Soil & waste combined in single stack
  • Simpler, economical
  • Higher risk of siphonage
  • Requires careful vent design
TWO-PIPE System
  • Separate stacks for soil & waste
  • Better sanitary protection
  • More expensive to install
  • Preferred for larger buildings
SINGLE STACK System
  • One pipe for all drainage + venting
  • All fixtures on same stack
  • Special single-stack fittings required
  • Used in high-rise buildings
Drainage Flow & DFU — Hydraulic Jump & Pipe Sizing
Key Hydraulic Concepts
HYDRAULIC JUMPSudden rise in liquid depth when fast flow meets slow in a horizontal pipe. Causes positive pressure fluctuations.
DFU (Drainage Fixture Unit)Unit measuring probable discharge into drainage system. Used to size drainage pipes.
Fixture BranchDrain pipe from fixture trap to soil/waste stack or building drain.
Grade (slope)Min 2% = 1:50 (2cm per meter) for pipes <75mm. Larger pipes: 1% acceptable.
Stack LoadingCombined DFU load on vertical stack. Determines stack size.
T&Y FittingsSanitary tee (T) & wye (Y) — direct horizontal to vertical connections. WYE preferred at base.

Cleanouts — Location Rules
When & Where to Place Cleanouts
1
At the upper end of every horizontal soil or waste pipe.
2
At every change of horizontal direction not less than 22.5°.
3
Within 1.5 m (5 ft) inside property line before house sewer connection.
4
At every 15 m (50 ft) of horizontal run of soil or waste pipe.
5
At base of every soil or waste stack.
Building Sewer, Sump & Sewage Disposal
Building Drain vs. Building Sewer
Building Drain (House Drain)Lowest horizontal piping inside the building. Receives all soil, waste & drain pipes inside. Extends to 0.60 m outside foundation wall.
Building Sewer (House Sewer)Starts 0.60 m from outside face of foundation. Goes to public sewer, septic, or disposal. Min diameter: 150mm. Not smaller than house drain.
Soil PipeConveys discharge of WC, urinals & similar fixtures to building drain/sewer.
Waste PipeCarries only waste water — NO fecal matter.
Sumps & Sewage Ejectors
Sump PitPit or tank receiving drainage from fixtures below public sewer level. Must be covered & vented.
Sewage EjectorPump that raises sewage from below-grade fixtures to building drain above sewer level.
Check ValveRequired on ejector discharge to prevent backflow.
VentSump must be vented separately — do NOT connect to regular drain vent system.
Fixtures below street sewer level require sewage ejector system.
Septic Tank — Private Sewage Disposal System
How a Septic Tank Works
1
Sewage enters septic tank from building sewer.
2
Separation: solids settle to bottom (sludge); grease floats to top (scum); clarified liquid in middle (effluent).
3
Digestion: anaerobic bacteria digest organic solids.
4
Effluent flows to leaching chamber / disposal field.
5
Soil absorption completes treatment via percolation.
Septic System Sizing
Use 6 DFU per water closet when computing septic tank size
Min. liquid capacity750 liters for 1–2 bedrooms; +200L per additional bedroom
Inlet & outletSubmerged inlet pipe; outlet tee extends into effluent zone
AccessMust have removable covers for inspection & cleaning
Distance from wellMin. 15m (50 ft) from any water supply
Rule of thumb: Disposal field area < 70 m². If exceeds, revise trench spacing.

Gray Water System
Gray Water — Definition & Sources
  • Water from lavatories, sinks, laundry trays & showers
  • Contains minor pollutants — NOT fecal matter
  • Separate from black water (WC & urinals — major pollutants)
  • Can be reclaimed after treatment for flushing, irrigation
Gray Water System Components
CollectionSeparate drainage from approved fixtures (lavatories, showers, laundry)
Holding TankStorage & settling before treatment or reuse
TreatmentSoil absorption, filtration or approved package treatment
IdentificationAll reclaimed water pipes must be clearly identified — different color/label
Cross-connectionAbsolutely PROHIBITED between potable water & gray water systems
Approved usesToilet flushing, sub-surface irrigation, cooling tower makeup

Pumps, HPS & Construction Management
Types of Pumps
Centrifugal Pumps
  • Most common in plumbing
  • Rotary impeller creates centrifugal force
  • Used for water supply & pressure boosting
  • Types: end-suction, split-case, turbine
  • Priming required (except self-priming type)
Submersible Pumps
  • Motor + pump submerged in fluid
  • Used for deep well, sump pits, sewage
  • No priming required
  • Hermetically sealed motor
  • Efficient — reduced friction losses
Reciprocating Pumps
  • Piston/plunger action — positive displacement
  • High pressure applications
  • Used for metering chemical dosing
  • Self-priming
  • Pulsating flow — needs dampener
Booster Pumps — Pressure Boosting System
  • Increase system pressure where mains pressure is insufficient
  • Typically centrifugal, multi-stage
  • Controlled by pressure switch or variable speed drive (VSD)
  • Hydropneumatic tank maintains pressure between pump cycles
  • Required when building height > pressure available from mains
Heat Exchangers & Hot Water Pumps
  • Circulating pump maintains hot water temperature in distribution loop
  • Prevents long wait for hot water at fixtures
  • Plate-type or shell-and-tube heat exchangers
  • Used in large buildings for hot water generation
  • Thermostatic mixing valve (TMV) blends hot & cold to safe temperature
Hydropneumatic (HPS) / Pressure Boosting Systems
HPS System Components
1
Pressure Tank (Hydropneumatic) — stores water under compressed air to maintain system pressure. Usually 1/3 water, 2/3 air.
2
Pressure Switch — starts pump at low-pressure setpoint (cut-in), stops at high-pressure setpoint (cut-out).
3
Check Valve — prevents backflow from tank into pump during off-cycle.
4
Relief Valve — safety device to prevent over-pressure conditions.
5
Air Volume Control — maintains proper air-water ratio inside tank.
HPS vs. Overhead Tank
HPS AdvantageEliminates overhead tank; provides constant pressure; space-saving; no contamination risk from open tank
HPS DisadvantagePower-dependent; pump failure = no water; higher energy use
Overhead TankGravity-fed; simple; power outage resilience; requires elevated structure
Tank-less BoosterVariable speed pump + pressure sensor — eliminates pressure tank; most energy-efficient modern system
Construction Management
Organization & Coordination
  • Inter-organization relations: Coordination between main contractor, plumbing contractor & other trades
  • Role of Engineer-in-Charge: Safety & security; approving materials; issuing variation orders
  • Working at heights & confined spaces: Permits to work; safety harnesses; ventilation required
  • Accident reporting: Incident reports submitted within 24hrs; near-miss records
  • Coordination with structural (pipe sleeves, penetrations), architectural (fixture placement), MEP (pipe routing)
Inventory & Material Management
  • Ordering & Stacking: First-in-first-out (FIFO); pipe stored horizontally; fittings in bins
  • Record keeping: Delivery receipts; material test certs; issuance logs
  • Measurement: Quantities verified against BOQ before installation
  • Billing: Progress billing tied to work done (% completion)
  • Time & Cost Analysis: Planned vs. actual tracking; cost codes per system
Specifications, BOQ & Cost Estimates
Bill of Quantities (BOQ)
TakeoffMeasure all quantities from drawings — pipe lengths, fittings count, fixture count
ItemizationList by system: water supply, drainage, sanitary, hot water, storm drainage
Unit ratesMaterial + labor per unit; labor often quoted per linear meter (pipes) or per unit (fixtures)
SummarySubtotal per system + overhead + profit + contingency (5–10%)
Specifications Writing — Key Contents
  • Scope of work — what is included/excluded
  • Referenced standards — NBC 2016, NPC 1999, UIPC 2021
  • Materials — approved pipe types, fittings, fixtures brands
  • Workmanship — jointing methods, pressure testing requirements
  • Testing & commissioning — hydrostatic test pressures, durations
  • Submittals — shop drawings, product data, operation manuals
Activity Schedule & Bar Chart (Gantt)
Construction Activity Sequence — Plumbing Works
1
Underground rough-in — install building drain, house sewer, storm drain pipes before slab pour. Pressure test before concealing.
2
Slab penetrations & sleeves — place pipe sleeves, waterproofing at penetrations, set floor drain bodies in slab formwork.
3
Rough-in (wall & ceiling) — install soil stacks, waste stacks, vent stacks, water supply risers, hot water piping within walls/ceiling before plastering.
4
Pressure testing — air or water test on drainage at 1.0 m water column; water supply at 1.5× operating pressure for minimum 2 hours before closing up.
5
Fixture setting — install fixtures, connections & trim after tiles/finishes complete. Water closets, lavatories, sinks, shower valves.
6
Commissioning & testing — flush tests, flow tests, check all isolating valves, functional test all fixtures. Document results.
7
Inspection & handover — final inspection by RMP (Registered Master Plumber); as-built drawings; O&M manuals submitted.
